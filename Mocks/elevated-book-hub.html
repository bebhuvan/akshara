About This Work

Originally serialized in Gujarati in Gandhi's weekly journal Navajivan between 1925 and 1929, My Experiments with Truth stands as one of the most influential autobiographies of the 20th century. Gandhi chronicles his life from his birth in 1869 through 1921, offering an unflinchingly honest account of his moral and spiritual development.

The autobiography reveals Gandhi's transformation from a shy, uncertain youth to the leader of India's independence movement. He writes candidly about his failures and mistakes—his struggles with physical desire, his early vanity, his experiments with diet and lifestyle—presenting them not as confessions but as lessons learned in his lifelong pursuit of truth and self-improvement.

Written during his imprisonment, the work reflects Gandhi's belief that one's personal life cannot be separated from one's public actions. His concept of satyagraha and his commitment to non-violence emerge not as abstract political philosophies but as hard-won insights derived from personal experience and spiritual practice.

Mahadev Desai's English translation, first published in 1927, maintains the directness and simplicity of Gandhi's prose while making his ideas accessible to a global audience. The work profoundly influenced leaders and activists worldwide, from Martin Luther King Jr. to Nelson Mandela, establishing Gandhi as a moral exemplar whose methods transcended cultural and historical boundaries.
